How AI Builds Your Workout Plan

April 26, 2026

Writing your own workout program is fine until you stall. And you will stall. The weights stop going up, the routine gets stale, and you are left guessing whether you should change exercises, add volume, deload, or just push through.

Automated programming removes that guesswork. Here is how the plan generator in LiftZone actually works, without the marketing talk.

It starts with a library of 289 exercises

Every exercise in the database is tagged with equipment requirements, primary and secondary muscle groups, and movement patterns. When the algorithm builds a plan, it pulls from this library based on what equipment you have available and what muscles need to be trained that day.

If you mark that you do not have a cable machine, the algorithm will not program cable flyes. It swaps in dumbbell flyes or another chest movement that works with what you have. You can also swap any exercise yourself after the plan is generated — tap the swap icon and pick from alternatives that target the same muscle group.

How it estimates your strength

The system needs to know how strong you are to prescribe the right weights. It uses the Epley formula, which is one of the most widely used strength estimation methods in exercise science:

Estimated 1-Rep Max = Weight x (1 + 0.0333 x Reps)

So if you bench press 80 kg for 8 reps, your estimated one-rep max is about 101 kg. The formula caps at 15 reps because sets above that start skewing the estimate — high-rep sets test endurance more than maximum strength.

The app scans your last 90 days of workout history to find your best estimated 1RM for each exercise. When you hit a new personal record, it updates automatically and adjusts your future sessions.

It even connects related exercises

Here is a detail most apps miss: exercises are related. If your flat bench press goes up, your incline bench and dumbbell press should go up too. LiftZone maintains a map of exercise relationships with multipliers so that a PR on one lift updates the baseline estimates for related movements.

All estimated weights are rounded to the nearest 2.5 kg because that is the smallest plate increment in most gyms. No one wants to see a prescription of 67.3 kg.

Three goal profiles, three different approaches

The plan changes significantly based on your goal:

  • Strength: 3 sets of 5 reps with 120 seconds of rest between sets. Heavy weights, low reps, full recovery.
  • Muscle growth: 3 sets of 10 reps with 90 seconds rest. Moderate weight, moderate volume, enough rest to maintain intensity.
  • Fat loss: 4 sets of 12 reps with 60 seconds rest. Lighter weight, higher volume, shorter rest to keep your heart rate elevated.

These are not arbitrary numbers. They come from well-established strength and hypertrophy research. The rest periods matter as much as the sets and reps — cutting rest from 120 to 60 seconds fundamentally changes the training stimulus.

Plans that do not run out

The algorithm generates 42 days of programming at a time. When you get down to 14 days remaining, it automatically extends the plan with new sessions based on your updated performance data. You never open the app and find an empty schedule.

Each session estimates how long it will take based on the number of exercises (roughly 8 minutes each plus transitions), warmup time (5 minutes), and cooldown (5 minutes). A 6-exercise strength session comes out to about 60 minutes. A 10-exercise fat-loss circuit runs closer to 75.

You can still customize everything

Automated does not mean rigid. You can adjust:

  • Session duration (45, 60, 75, or 90+ minutes)
  • Rest period multiplier (0.75x for faster sessions, 1.5x for more recovery)
  • Custom sets and reps (override the goal defaults)
  • Training days per week
  • Muscle group focus
  • Equipment availability

Change any of these and the plan regenerates around your new constraints. It is a system that adapts to your life, not the other way around.
